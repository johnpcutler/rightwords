Part 2: Why Kanban Works Better In Manufacturing Than In Knowledge Work

This note builds on the distinction between endurants and perdurants.

  • An endurant is the thing that persists: a part, a batch, a machine, a ticket, a request.
  • A perdurant is the flow, process, or unfolding activity through time.

The core claim here is simple:

Kanban works best when both the thing and the flow are stable, and when the thing's position in the flow reliably represents reality.

Or even shorter:

Kanban assumes nouns that behave and verbs that repeat.

If you come from Kanban practice rather than ontology:

  • the card or item is the endurant
  • the flow of work is the perdurant
  • movement across the board is a set of transition events

First Principles

Kanban is not just a board. At first principles, it is a system for:

  • controlling flow
  • of things
  • through repeatable stages

So it assumes:

  • you have identifiable things
  • those things move through a reasonably consistent process
  • the mapping between the thing and the flow state is predictable

That last point matters most. Kanban is not just assuming stability in the abstract. It is assuming that the thing and the flow reinforce each other.

Manufacturing

In manufacturing, the endurants are usually stable:

  • a part is a part
  • a unit is a unit
  • a batch is a batch

There may be edge cases, but most of the time people are not arguing about what the thing is.

The perdurants are also usually stable:

  • the process steps are known
  • the steps repeat
  • the process does not radically morph mid-stream

For example:

  • Cut
  • Assemble
  • Inspect
  • Ship

And this is the key:

The state of the endurant is closely tied to its position in the perdurant.

If a part is in Inspection, that usually means something real and consistent. So:

  • the column is meaningful
  • the movement is meaningful
  • WIP limits are meaningful

In other words, Kanban works in manufacturing not just because the parts are stable and the process is stable independently, but because the relationship between them is tight.

Strong Coupling

In manufacturing:

  • the endurant is real
  • the perdurant is real
  • the coupling between them is strong

That means the board can act as a useful operational model of reality.

When the card moves, something real has happened.

When the queue grows, it usually signals something real.

When WIP is too high, it usually points to an actual flow problem.

Knowledge Work

Software teams often borrow the same structure:

  • tickets or stories play the role of endurants
  • columns play the role of perdurant stages

But the stability is weaker on both sides.

The endurants are shaky:

  • what exactly is a ticket?
  • what is a feature?
  • where does one piece of work end and another begin?
  • scope changes constantly

The perdurants are shaky too:

  • stages are inconsistently applied
  • work jumps around
  • discovery changes the path
  • loops and rework are common

The result is that the coupling breaks down.

The column no longer reliably reflects reality.

Examples:

  • In Progress can mean ten different things
  • Done may hide unresolved issues
  • Blocked may describe politics, not just technical dependency

So the board still has value, but it becomes a lossy representation rather than a tight operational mirror.

Reliable Mapping

The important distinction is not simply:

  • manufacturing is stable
  • knowledge work is unstable

The deeper point is:

Kanban works when the mapping between thing and flow state is reliable.

So:

  • in manufacturing, the mapping is usually strong
  • in knowledge work, the mapping is often weak

That is why a Kanban board in manufacturing often feels literal, while a Kanban board in knowledge work often feels interpretive.

Ontology

This fits directly with the earlier ontology distinction.

In manufacturing:

  • the endurant is real: part, unit, batch
  • the perdurant is real: the production process
  • the relationship is tight

In knowledge work:

  • the endurant is often partly constructed: ticket, initiative, request
  • the perdurant is messy, evolving, and discovery-heavy
  • the relationship is loose and lossy

So the problem is not that Kanban is wrong. The problem is that it depends on a kind of ontological stability that many knowledge-work settings only partially have.

Bounded Flow

Kanban works best inside bounded, stable flows.

It works extremely well inside a bounded, stable flow. It assumes:

  • reasonably consistent intake
  • reasonably consistent work types
  • reasonably consistent process

But it says much less about:

  • where work comes from
  • how work changes shape upstream
  • how meaning evolves during discovery

Those are exactly the places where the perdurant is unstable and the coupling between thing and flow starts to break.

This is also why Kanban often gets harder as you move upstream or outward.

At the team level, a board can still be a useful model of flow.

At the portfolio level, or in highly autonomous environments, the board often starts mixing:

  • incoming demand
  • self-generated work
  • strategy
  • politics
  • dependencies
  • changing definitions of the work itself

At that point, the board may still be useful, but it is no longer a simple left-to-right picture of reality.

Clean Version

Here is the clean version:

Kanban works best when both the thing and the flow are stable, and when a thing's position in the flow reliably represents reality.

And the even tighter version:

Kanban assumes nouns that behave and verbs that repeat.

Contrast

The contrast becomes sharp:

  • factories: nouns are real, verbs are repeatable, so Kanban thrives
  • knowledge work: nouns are approximations, verbs are evolving, so Kanban strains

This does not mean Kanban is useless in knowledge work. It means its fit is uneven and context-dependent.

Where It Still Works

Even in knowledge work, Kanban can work very well in bounded areas where the coupling becomes stronger again.

Examples:

  • support queues
  • service desks
  • standardized review pipelines
  • repetitive operational workflows
  • content production pipelines with repeatable stages

In those settings, both the thing and the flow become more stable, so the board becomes more truthful.

More generally, Kanban is useful when it is used for one or more of these jobs:

  • making queueing visible
  • exposing bottlenecks
  • limiting WIP
  • improving handoffs
  • stabilizing a repeatable service flow

It is less useful when people expect the same board to explain strategy formation, political influence, customer context, and evolving meaning all at once.

In those cases, the board is usually carrying more than one system at the same time.

A Simple Test

This matters because it gives you a sharper lens for deciding when to trust a board.

Ask:

  • is the thing actually stable?
  • is the flow actually stable?
  • does board position really correspond to reality?

If the answer is yes, Kanban can be powerful.

If the answer is mostly no, the board may still be useful, but it should be treated as a rough coordination device rather than an accurate model of work.

And in some contexts, it is better treated as a living map than as a pipeline:

  • useful for surfacing what is happening
  • useful for conversation and coordination
  • less reliable as a precise model of how work actually unfolds

Try This Now

  • Pick one workflow in your organization where a board or flow model works surprisingly well.
  • Then pick one where the same approach starts to feel thin, misleading, or overly neat.
  • Ask: which assumptions hold in the first case and break in the second?
